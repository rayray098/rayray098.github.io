Health Effects

Confining a large number of animals in a small land area can be seriously hazardous for their well-being. Poor ventilation causes a buildup of toxic gases such as ammonia, hydrogen sulfide, and methane. These gases can irritate animals’ eyes and lungs or even become toxic. Ammonia also damages respiratory tissues, making animals more vulnerable to infections. Crowded environments can also increase aggressive behavior, which may lead to broken bones, bruises, cuts, and sometimes death.

Crowding, stress, and poor air quality are major welfare concerns in CAFOs.

Why This Matters

CAFOs are designed for efficiency, but the tradeoff is often lower animal welfare. When animals cannot move freely or live in cleaner, more natural conditions, their physical health and behavior can both suffer.
